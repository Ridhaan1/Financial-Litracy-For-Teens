Broke by Sunday: Why Does Your Money Disappear

By Ridhaan Katraria, Founder & Editor

It happens to everyone, me, you, our friends, literally every single person. Every time I get money from my grandparents, I end up spending it on eating out, going out with friends, and stuff like that. A little here, a little there, and before you even realize it, the money is just gone. The funny part is it's gone in like 5 days, and then I have to wait an entire month just to get that money again. And the worst part? You can't even really explain where it went. You didn't buy anything huge. There was no one big purchase you can point to. It just sort of... disappeared into snacks, outings, and random things you probably didn't even need.

And while our parents scold us for not saving money, they don't really teach us how to do it either. They tell us to save, but nobody sits us down and explains what that actually means in practice. How much do you save? From what? Where does it go? What's it even for? We're expected to just figure it out, and most of us don't, at least not until we've already made a bunch of expensive mistakes.

The truth is, managing money isn't something you just naturally know how to do. It's a skill, and like any skill, someone has to teach it to you. Since most schools don't cover it and most parents assume someone else will, a lot of teenagers enter adulthood completely unprepared. So that's where budgeting comes in. There are simple, proven methods that anyone can start using right now, no finance degree required. Two of the most popular ones are the three jar method and the 50/30/20 rule.

The Three Jar Method

The three jar method is exactly what it sounds like. You take whatever money you have and split it into three separate categories before you spend a single rupee of it. The act of splitting it first is what makes the difference.

The first jar is for spending. This is your everyday money for food, hanging out, small stuff you want to use right away. No guilt here, this is meant to be spent. The second jar is for saving, money you put aside for something specific, like a new phone, a trip, a gadget, or whatever goal you're actually working toward. Seeing that number grow week by week makes saving feel real instead of abstract. The third jar is for emergencies, money you don't touch unless something actually comes up, an unexpected expense, something breaks, or you genuinely need it. Think of it as your safety net.

A good starting split is something like 50% spending, 30% saving, and 20% emergency. But the exact numbers matter less than actually splitting it in the first place. The moment you divide your money into categories, you stop spending all of it just because it's sitting there. You go from "I have ₹2,000" to "I have ₹1,000 to spend, ₹600 going toward my goal, and ₹400 I'm not touching." That shift in thinking is everything.

The 50/30/20 Rule

The 50/30/20 rule works on a similar idea but is a bit more structured. 50% of your money goes toward needs, things you genuinely have to spend on. 30% goes toward wants, the fun stuff, eating out, entertainment, things you enjoy but don't strictly need. And 20% goes straight into savings. This rule is used by adults managing full salaries, but it works just as well for pocket money or allowance. The principle is the same: give every rupee a job before you spend it.

Why This Matters

This matters because budgeting isn't just a life skill, it's a life saver. And if we don't focus on it right now, it'll definitely impact us in the long run. Right now the stakes are low. If you mess up your budget this week, worst case you're out of snack money for a few days. But one day it won't be allowance or pocket money, it'll be an actual salary, rent, bills, and real financial responsibility. And the mistakes that come with not knowing how to manage money get a lot more expensive when that happens.

The teenagers who build this habit now are the ones who don't end up stressed about money later. Not because they earn more, but because they know what to do with what they have. That's the real advantage. It's not about being rich, it's about being in control.

Try It

Next time you get your money, don't call for a McDonald's party. Before you spend a single rupee, split it. Decide what's going where. Give your money a plan. Implement the method that actually helps you in the long run, unlike junk food. Your future self will thank you for it.
